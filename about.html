PHILIPPINES

The Pearl of the Orient Seas

LANGUAGE

For more than three centuries, Spanish served as the country's official language. In the early 20th century, it was the primary, second, or third language used by 60% of the populace. However, with American control in the early 1900s, Spanish usage started to drop. English and Spanish were designated as the official languages of the Philippines in the 1935 Constitution. The Tagalog language was selected as the national tongue in 1939. In 1959, the language was renamed "Pilipino," then in 1973, it was renamed "Filipino." English and Filipino are designated as shared official languages in the current Constitution.

CULTURE

The Philippines is a nation with many different cultural influences. The majority of these effects are byproducts of earlier colonialism, mostly coming from Spanish and American cultures. The traditional Asian culture of the Filipinos has been preserved and is evident in their way of life, beliefs, and rituals amid all of these influences. Filipino culture is visible everywhere you go and has generally been valued and even praised in many areas of the world.

MUSIC, ARTS, AND LITERATURE

Filipinos love music a much. They produce sound using a variety of materials. During festive occasions, they enjoy singing in groups and dancing (Tiniking and Carinosa). They were introduced to a range of musical instruments, including the violin, ukulele, trumpet, drums, and more, by Spanish settlers. The majority of their music is modern, and they have also mastered the art of creating original songs based on actual occurrences. Folklore, influenced by Spanish literature and the early church, is also popular. The national hero of the Philippines, Jose Rizal, is renowned for his literary works and novels that drew inspiration from the nation's struggle for independence.

RELIGION

Christian faith is practiced by the vast majority of Filipinos. Spain had a significant impact on the populace, and as a result, the Philippines and East Timor are the only two predominantly Christian countries in the Asia Pacific region. According to Wikipedia, over 80% of Filipinos identify as Christians (primarily Catholics), 11% identify as Muslims, and 9% identify with various religions and beliefs.

CELEBRATIONS

One of the most cherished holidays in the Philippines is Christmas. On December 24, families and friends get together to savor meals made for "Noche Buena," a Spanish phrase that translates to "midnight dinner" to welcome Christmas Day. Another holiday that brings together Filipino families is New Year's. One of the numerous Filipino rituals is donning dotted clothing and setting round fruits on the table to represent wealth.

FAMILY STRUCTURE

The family is the fundamental social unit of the nation, which also includes extended family (aunts, uncles, grandparents, cousins) and other outside relationships (godparents and close friends). As a result, many kids have many godparents, and grandparents typically take care of kids when their parents have to travel for business. Members of the same family frequently work for the same business, a practice popularized by the first Chinese immigrants to the Philippines. Filipino families reside in various housing types depending on their position or location. Families in rural regions reside in nipa huts constructed of bamboo with corrugated metal roofs or palm leaves for walls. The "middle-class" Filipinos live in homes built of bricks and stones.

MEALS

Even though their small stature may not make it clear, Filipinos are tremendous eaters. Because of the distinctiveness and variety of its cuisine, the Philippines is referred to be Asia's melting pot. Filipinos are unable to eat without rice on any given day. They enjoy eating pork, chicken, and salted fish with simple rice. The several foods they have learned to make and consume are served after the rice. The typical Filipino diet consists of five daily meals: breakfast, a mid-morning snack, merienda, lunch, and supper.
